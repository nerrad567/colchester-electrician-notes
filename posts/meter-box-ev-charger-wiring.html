Meter boxes · EV charging · BS 7671

Why you shouldn’t put customer wiring inside a meter box

Written by Darren Gray · Gray Logic Electrical · Colchester

Quick answers · Common myths

Common misunderstandings (the short version)

  • “It’s locked, so it’s safe.” A lock controls access. It doesn’t prove the enclosure is suitable to provide basic protection for customer wiring.
  • “The homeowner owns the meter box.” Ownership doesn’t change what the enclosure was designed to do. Owning a gas meter box doesn’t make it a boiler cupboard.
  • “Supplier isolators are in there, so anything goes.” Supplier gear is installed under supplier authority as part of the metering arrangement. That doesn’t give a free pass for customer equipment.
  • “It looks tidy.” Tidy isn’t the same as suitable. Use a purpose-made enclosure for customer kit.

The practical rule I work to

If you need to add customer equipment near the intake (EV isolator, small board, junctioning, etc.), fit a separate enclosure next to the meter position that’s designed to house live parts and is rated for the environment. Don’t repurpose the meter cabinet.

I’m seeing more domestic meter boxes used as a “handy spare cupboard” for customer wiring: EV charger feeds, isolators, small distribution boxes, and junctions added wherever there’s space.

It often looks neat. It’s outside and out of the way. And because many meter boxes are owned by the homeowner, it’s easy to assume:

“If it fits inside the meter box, it must be OK.”

In practice, it’s a source of problems — sometimes immediately, but more often later when equipment is upgraded, faults develop, or a meter operator needs access.

What a domestic meter box is actually for

A GRP meter cabinet is intended to house the intake and metering arrangement: service head / cut-out, meter, and sometimes a supplier-installed isolator.

It’s designed for weather protection, basic security, and routine access for meter readers and service engineers. It is not generally designed, declared, or assessed as a wiring enclosure for customer circuits.

That last sentence matters. A box can be “an enclosure” in the everyday sense, but that doesn’t automatically make it the sort of enclosure BS 7671 expects you to rely on for protecting people from contact with live parts.

Locks and keys don’t automatically make it suitable

A common argument is that the meter box is locked or needs a key to open.

Access control helps, but it doesn’t answer the real question: is this enclosure intended to act as the protective barrier for live customer wiring?

Meter boxes are routinely opened by people who are there to deal with metering equipment — not to encounter customer-installed wiring arrangements, junctions, or boards.

“But the supplier put an isolator in there…”

Supplier isolators are often fitted inside meter cabinets and can look similar to customer equipment. The difference isn’t how it looks — it’s responsibility and purpose.

Supplier equipment is installed under supplier authority as part of the metering arrangement. Customer equipment isn’t. The presence of supplier gear doesn’t create a general permission to treat the meter cabinet as a customer wiring space.

Why adding a separate enclosure is fine (and usually best)

None of this means you can’t add equipment near the intake.

The clean solution is to add a separate, purpose-made enclosure next to or below the meter cabinet — for example a small EV distribution enclosure or an isolator enclosure rated for outdoor use.

These enclosures are designed to house live parts, provide a defined protective barrier, and be installed and maintained as part of the customer’s electrical installation.

Put simply: you can add a proper enclosure — you shouldn’t repurpose the meter cabinet.

A very common EV charger trap: SWA termination inside the meter box

One setup I’m seeing a lot is an EV charger supply routed through the meter cabinet. The cable is often SWA (steel wire armoured), and the termination into a small enclosure is sometimes done with a stuffing gland rather than a proper armour-clamping gland.

Most EV chargers are plastic. That means the armour is often not properly dealt with at the charger end (or it’s assumed “it’ll be fine”).

If the armour isn’t correctly terminated and earthed at at least one end, you can end up with a floating conductive layer. That’s not the sort of thing you want hidden in a cabinet that gets opened for metering work.

Why this matters in practice

This isn’t about paperwork. It’s about future work and future safety.

  • Meter operators and DNO/supplier engineers need clear access to metering equipment.
  • Extra customer kit inside the meter cabinet often leads to delays, refusal to work, or “clear it first” conversations.
  • Poor termination practices (especially on EV supplies) can sit unnoticed until something goes wrong.

The takeaway

  • Meter cabinets are for the metering arrangement — not a general customer wiring enclosure.
  • A lock doesn’t automatically make an enclosure suitable to rely on for customer wiring protection.
  • Supplier equipment doesn’t mean “anything goes”.
  • If you need to add customer equipment at the intake, fit a separate enclosure that’s designed for it.
